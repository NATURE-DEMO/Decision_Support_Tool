The final step of the Level 3 risk assessment is the adaptation module, which is designed to support informed decision-making by evaluating and prioritizing feasible adaptation options. The adaptation process at Level 3 aims to:
• Prioritize adaptation options that maximize risk reduction, particularly in high-risk segments or assets identified through high-resolution analysis.
• Account for economic, technical, social and environmental factors that influence implementation feasibility.
• Incorporate co-benefits and residual risk into the final decision-making process.
• Compare the effectiveness of grey infrastructure, nature-based solutions (NbS), and governance - planning measures in a transparent, evidence-based manner.

For the Globocica Dam demo site the assessment is conducted with special focus on the evaluation of adaptation options for impacts related to heavy precipitation, landslide and rockfall. Based on the RCP8.5 climate scenario and the 2050-time horizon and taking into account the previously calculated PRI values (see Table 7.26, a set of potential adaptation interventions aimed at reducing the infrastructure’s vulnerability and risk exposure are identified. The Nature-based Solutions proposed here are extracted from the NbS Catalog developed under Work Package 1 (D1.1, WP1). For the Globocica Dam demo site, a set of NbS interventions specifically applicable to landslide and rockfall stability risk are considered. These recommended solutions are presented in Table 7.27 and include measures such as afforestation and reforestation, terracing, hydroand mulch seeding, and other live layered techniques intended to stabilize slopes, reduce runoff-induced erosion, and promote ecosystem-based risk attenuation. The evaluation of the suggested adaptation measures, whether NbS or grey infrastructure options, is conducted through a Multi-Criteria Analysis (MCA), as outlined in Deliverable D.2.1. This approach allows for a comprehensive and balanced assessment of each option, considering a range of qualitative and quantitative criteria beyond risk reduction alone.

The MCA framework incorporates key evaluation dimensions such as:
• Effectiveness in reducing the Potential Risk Index (PRI).
• Implementation cost.
• Feasibility and technical applicability.
• Environmental and social co-benefits.
• Long-term sustainability and maintenance requirements.

The Co-benefit Factor (CBF), on the other hand, specifically captures the non-risk-related advantages of each option, primarily environmental and social benefits. It is calculated through the Environmental Impact score and Social Acceptance score, as outlined in Deliverable D.2.1. These weights reflect the methodological emphasis placed on environmental performance, while also recognizing the importance of social alignment and stakeholder support. From the results shown in Table 3.26, it is observed that all proposed NbS options demonstrate high levels of environmental and social acceptability (scores of 4 or 5). This is combined with strong cobenefit factors ranging from 3.5 to 5, reaffirming the added value of these options beyond risk reduction alone. Among the options, AO01 (FW1 – Afforestation and Reforestation) is the most suitable overall, with a suitability score of 4.2, reflecting a well-balanced profile across all criteria: strong technical efficiency, reasonable implementation cost and time, and excellent environmental and social co-benefits. AO01 (FW1) also achieves the highest co-benefit factor of 5. AO05 (SWB1 - Hydro- and mulch seeding) also scores 4.2, but with a slightly lower co-benefit factor of 4.4. This indicates good suitability in terms of implementation cost, time and environmental benefits, but lower technical efficiency. The reason lies in its primary function as a solution tackling erosion control by addressing the soil surface, rather than mitigating rockfall. Therefore, AO05 is most effective when applied in combination with other adaptation options, such as afforestation and reforestation (AO01, FW1). AO04 (FW2 – Protection Forest Management) follows closely with a score of 4, performing similarly to AO01 in technical and co-benefit terms, but requiring slightly longer implementation time. AO06 (SWB5 – Live staking) and AO07 (SWB6 – Live fencing) both score 3.8, largely due to moderate technical efficiency and higher implementation demands. Despite this, their consistently high environmental and social scores make them valuable options. AO08 (SWB8 – Live layering techniques) scores slightly lower at 3.4, primarily due to cost and implementation time, yet it maintains strong environmental and social performance, indicating strong potential in areas where public acceptance and ecological restoration are prioritized over rapid deployment. AO02 (SE1 – Terracing); and AO03 (SE4 – 3D steel grids with vegetation) have suitability scores of 3. Although technically efficient (scores of 4), their high costs and long implementation times significantly reduce their practicality under current resource and time constraints.
