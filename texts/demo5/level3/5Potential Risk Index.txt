The fifth stage of the Level 3 high-resolution climate risk assessment involves the computation of the Potential Risk Index (PRI), which integrates the results of the Hazard Index (HI), Exposure Index (EI), and Vulnerability Index (VI) into a single risk value. The PRI values are calculated separately for mid-term (2050) and long-term (2100) horizons under two representative concentration pathways (RCP4.5 and RCP8.5), but presented results focus on the RCP8.5 scenario in 2050 only. The results for the PRI for Level 3 calculation for the Globocica Dam demo site are presented in Table below. The results from Level 3 risk assessment confirm that the highest risks are linked to precipitation-driven hazards, particularly heavy rainfall, erosion and landslides. The most severe case is observed for the dam, where reactive CAPEX due to spillway blockage reaches PRI=3, corresponding to a MEDIUM RISK level. This outcome reflects the combined effect of moderate-to-high hazard (HI=3), very high exposure (EI=5) and high vulnerability (VI=4) and highlights the critical importance of protecting the dam against this type of impact. For other assets, including the spillway chute and intake tunnel, as well as the operational road and building, precipitation-driven impacts are associated with PRI=2 (LOW RISK). These hazards still have non-negligible consequences for CAPEX, OPEX, and revenues, mainly through sedimentation, reservoir capacity reduction, and rockfall-induced damage. Temperature-related hazards, including cold waves and frost, generate VERY LOW RISK (PRI=1), primarily causing operational delays and ice-related interruptions to the service road and building. While less severe than hydrological extremes, they remain relevant for long-term performance and maintenance costs.

1. Landslide and Rockfall as Primary Hazards: Slope-related processes, triggered mainly by heavy precipitation, such as landslide and rockfall dominate the risk profile. The dam shows the highest potential risk (PRI=3), highlighting its vulnerability to landslide-induced slope instability and debris accumulation. The intake tunnel and spillway chute show lower risk (PRI=2) to rockfall from unstable formations located directly above the chute, whichmay block or damage the system and compromise its discharge capacities. The service road and operational building also have potential risk values of PRI=2 due to their location near unstable slopes prone to rock and debris fall, which could affect structural integrity and access. Although these risks are generally below critical thresholds, their persistent and cumulative nature could lead to deterioration and blockage of normal operations of the system. This reinforces the need for preventive slope stabilization with long-term solutions such as afforestation in loose-material zones, as well as spillway protection measures.

2. Heavy Precipitation as a Persistent Trigger: Heavy rainfall is the main trigger of slope instability processes and related hazards (point 1). All critical assets register PRI values of 2-3, reflecting the potential for erosion, debris washouts, drainage blockages and sedimentation in the spillway. Such processes not only destabilize the terrain but also reduce the effectiveness of existing protective measures, further threatening the long-term resilience of the site. Though not severe on their own, these hazards are persistent and recurrent, amplifying landslide and rockfall risks and increasing maintenance and operational costs.

3. Low Risks from Temperature-Related Hazards: Cold waves and frost events show low risk (PRI=1) for the intake tunnel, service road and service building. These scores indicate the limited severity of projected cold extremes. Still, isolated cases of frost-induced damage or increased maintenance demands during snow and ice events cannot be fully ignored.

4. Operational Risks Parallel Structural Risks: Operational disruptions largely mirror the structural risk profile. For example, the intake tunnel (PRI=2 for precipitation- and landslide-driven hazards) demonstrates how moderate structural impacts, such as blockages or debris blockage, can quickly lead to reduced discharge capacity and interruptions in site operations. The dam, with PRI=3 implies that operational risk related to any structural compromise have broader implications for reservoir function and can extend to reservoir safety, electricity production and potentially the stability of the public network balance. Furthermore, rockfall- or landslide-induced damage to the spillway structures and service road not only limits site functionality but can also lead to indirect impacts, affecting adjacent infrastructure, transport routes and local communities. This alignment between structural integrity and operational risks underscores the interdependence between asset condition and service continuity.
