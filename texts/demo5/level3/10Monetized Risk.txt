The sixth and final stage of the Level 3 high-resolution risk assessment methodology consists of the monetization of climate risk. This stage represents a significant advancement compared to Level 2, where quantification remained mostly qualitative or semi-quantitative. By translating climate risk into monetary terms, decision-makers gain a clearer understanding of the potential financial consequences of climate impacts on critical infrastructure, supporting cost–benefit analyses, investment prioritization, and risk-informed planning. The monetized risk assessment translates the results of the Hazard Index (HI), Exposure Index (EI), and Vulnerability Index (VI) into direct financial terms by combining infrastructure valuation with damage estimation. Each asset is economically characterized through CAPEX, OPEX, and, where applicable, revenue values. These are then linked to hazard-specific monetization curves, which express the percentage of expected financial loss for a given level of climate hazard intensity. Finally, the Monetized Potential Risk (MPR) is calculated by multiplying the asset value by the estimated damage percentage, providing results in million euros (MEUR). This process allows climate risks to be expressed in comparable economic terms, supporting cost–benefit analysis, prioritization of adaptation options, and evidence-based resilience planning. For the Globocica Dam demo site, the analysis has been applied for all exposed assets in the dam system. The results are summarized in Table 7.26, which presents the expected financial losses (in million euros, MEUR) under the RCP8.5 scenario for the 2050-time horizon.The monetized risk assessment results provide a comprehensive view of how climate-driven hazards at the Globocica Dam demo site translate into economic consequences across its main exposed assets. Most single-impact events result in relatively modest financial consequences, ranging from around €0.001 and €2 million. However, the dam itself shows substantially higher exposure and much larger monetized risk, as heavy-precipitation-induced spillway blockage could generate losses of up to €23.5 million. This figure far exceeds risks to other infrastructure and underscores the dam’s pivotal role in system functionality and indicates where adaptation measures would yield the highest avoided-loss benefits. The intake tunnel and spillway chute are subject to recurring, medium-scale monetized risks that reflect the area’s exposure to landslide and rockfall activity. For example, sedimentation-related disruption of the tunnel could produce the largest non-dam revenue loss of €1.9 million, while the chute damage due to rockfall-induced damage could cost €2 million. These values are smaller compared to those of the dam but point to recurring threats to operational continuity under heavy precipitation scenarios, particularly as protective meshes and retaining structures provide only partial and time-limited defence against slope instability. The service road and operational building show much smaller monetized impacts, generally below €0.001 - €1.47m. Though individually small, such impacts represent vulnerabilities that, if unmanaged, could escalate into greater maintenance concerns and reduce reliability of the whole system. The main financial insights from the monetized analysis are:

1. Dam-Related CAPEX Losses Dominate Financial Exposure: The dam is the primary driver of financial exposure, with spillway blockage under heavy precipitation leading to a potential €23.52 million in repair costs (PRI=3). This risk is connected to heavy precipitation-induced landslide and rockfall, which pose a risk to the stability of the surrounding terrain and can lead to deposition of debris and soil near the dam. Such events may block safe discharge of water or damage the spillway chute and intake tunnel. These impacts could compromise the functioning of the entire dam system through reduction or halt of power production, destabilization of the public network balance, and cut-off of transportation routes for the nearby population. This clearly outweighs monetized risks across other assets, underscoring the dam’s critical role in ensuring the overall system resilience. It also highlights the financial importance and need for preventive measures, such as slope stabilization, sedimentation control and debris management as priority measures to safeguard both the dam and the connected infrastructure it supports.

2. Medium-Scale Recurrent Risks from Slope Instability on critical assets: Other assets, while less financially exposed, face recurring risks connected to slope instability. For instance, the intake tunnel faces revenue losses of around €1.9 million due to sedimentation, while the chute is at risk of €2.06 million from rockfall damage. These risks are heightened as the right-bank zone of the dam, located above the spillway chute and service road, is characterized by occasional rock and debris fall events. Existing protective mesh and retaining walls offer only partial and time-limited defence, meaning that the upper slope zone, consisting of unconsolidated colluvial material, remains particularly unstable and susceptible to sliding. Even though medium scale, they are recurring risks and underline the need for long-term solutions, such as afforestation to improve slope stability in the upper zones and potential installation of drainage systems to limit erosion.

3. Maintenance and Operational Costs Are Lower but Not Negligible: The operational road and service building register small to medium losses (<€0.001-€1.47 million), but these kinds of expenses, if left unaddressed, gradually accumulate and can strain operating budgets and reduce site functionality. Rockfall and frost-related delays on these assets are recurrent, especially during periods of heavy rainfall, freeze-thaw processes or seismic activity. While not critical for maintenance and operations, they still require periodic clearance and monitoring. Implementing these costs into routine operations ensures that small-scale risks do not accumulate into larger reliability issues.

4. Risk Differentiation Enables Strategic Prioritization: The identified gap between the dam’s exposure and that of other assets clarifies where investments should be concentrated. Immediate priorities are dam and spillway facilities against rockfall and landslide hazards directly above the chute, which can block discharge capacity, damage structures and trigger cascading failures. Secondary focus should be placed on limiting rockfall-induced impacts on the operational road and service building ensuring continuity through preventive maintenance. Lower-value risks, such as frost-related operational delays, while not critical, remain relevant for long-term planning and service reliability.

5. Gaps and Uncertainties in Monetized Value: Some risks, particularly those associated with temperature extremes or minor operational disruptions, appear negligible in financial terms. However, climate variability such as droughts, intense rainfall, snowfall, and freeze-thaw cycles is intensifying soil erosion and ground instability, which can increase the likelihood of landslides and rockfall beyond current estimates. Improving hazard-damage models, refining climate projections and periodically reassessing monetized values will improve accuracy and support better financial decision-making.
