The sixth and final stage of the Level 3 high-resolution risk assessment methodology consists of the monetization of climate risk. This stage represents a significant advancement compared to Level 2, where quantification remained mostly qualitative or semi-quantitative. By translating climate risk into monetary terms, decision-makers gain a clearer understanding of the potential financial consequences of climate impacts on critical infrastructure (CI), supporting cost–benefit analyses, investment prioritization, and risk-informed planning. The monetized risk assessment translates the results of the Hazard Index (HI), Exposure Index (EI), and Vulnerability Index (VI) into direct financial terms by combining infrastructure valuation with damage estimation. First, each segment’s assets are economically characterized through their CAPEX, OPEX, and, where applicable, revenue values. These are then linked to hazard-specific monetization curves, which express the percentage of expected financial loss for a given level of climate hazard intensity. Finally, the Monetized Potential Risk (MPR) is calculated by multiplying the asset value by the estimated damage percentage, providing results in million euros (MEUR). This process allows climate risks to be expressed in comparable economic terms, supporting cost–benefit analysis, prioritization of adaptation options, and evidence-based resilience planning. For the Zvolen case study, the analysis has been applied to the three localities identified in Level 3: S1 – Zvolen Bypass Corridor (E77), S2 – Pustýhrad slopes, and S3 – Neresnica.The monetized risk results provide a clear picture of how climate hazards translate into economic consequences for the Zvolen localities. Segment S2 (Pustý hrad slopes) stands out with the highest financial exposure, particularly from landslides and drought-induced soil instability, where combined CAPEX and revenue-related losses may exceed €6.5 million. This reflects both the vulnerability of cultural heritage assets and the critical role of power lines and slope stability in ensuring service continuity. Segment S1 (Zvolen Bypass Corridor (E77)) shows moderate but recurrent risks, with heavy rain and landslide-related CAPEX damages leading to losses around €1.2 million, complemented by operational disruptions of around €0.25 million. Meanwhile, S3 (Neresnica) registers lower absolute losses (around €1.2 million in total) but remains highly relevant given its exposure to localized culvert failures and slope instability that could isolate the corridor and disrupt traffic flow. Overall, the monetization stage highlights that while structural repair costs (CAPEX) and maintenance (OPEX) remain moderate in absolute terms, operational and service-related disruptions, particularly in S2, pose the greatest financial threat. These findings emphasize the necessity of targeted adaptation measures, with slope stabilization and vegetation management being urgent priorities in S2, while drainage upgrades and pavement reinforcement are key for S1 and S3.
