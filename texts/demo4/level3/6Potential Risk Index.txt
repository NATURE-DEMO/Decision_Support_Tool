The fifth stage of the Level 3 high-resolution climate risk assessment involves the computation of the Potential Risk Index (PRI), which integrates the results of the Hazard Index (HI), Exposure Index (EI), and Vulnerability Index (VI) into a single risk value. This approach, described in Deliverable D2.1, allows for a more detailed and spatially differentiated quantification of climate-related risks compared to Level 2. In the Zvolen case study, the analysis was performed for each of the three selected segments, i.e., S1 (Zvolen Bypass Corridor – E77), S2 (Pustý hrad slopes), and S3 (Neresnica, I/66), using high-resolution climate projections derived from the EURO-CORDEX datasets (10–50 km resolution). The PRI values are calculated separately for mid-term (2050) and long-term (2100) horizons under two representative concentration pathways (RCP4.5 and RCP8.5), but for illustrative purposes, the results presented here focus on the RCP8.5 scenario in 2050, which represents a more adverse climate future. The resulting PRI scores for each segment and asset are summarized. These tables consolidate the different impact types (damages, maintenance, and operational disruptions), the associated climate hazards and indicators, and the computed PRI values.Segment S1 (Zvolen Bypass Corridor (E77)) shows mainly low-to-moderate PRI values (2–3), particularly for heavy precipitation and heat-related impacts on the road surface and drainage systems, while risks from wind remain negligible. Segment S2 (Pustý hrad slopes) exhibits the highest PRI scores (up to 5), driven by the combination of steep forested terrain, susceptibility to landslides, and the presence of critical assets such as power lines and cultural heritage. Segment S3 (Neresnica – I/66) presents moderate PRI levels (2–3), with notable risks linked to landslides and heavy rain, largely due to the vulnerability of culverts and vegetated slopes. Across all segments, landslides and drought-related ground instability emerge as the dominant risks, while operational disruptions from wind events remain of relatively low significance. These findings confirm the importance of a segment-level approach in the Level 3 methodology, as it enables decision-makers to target adaptation measures to the specific local vulnerabilities and economic priorities along the Zvolen transport network. The PRI values derived for the Zvolen case study illustrate varying degrees of climate-related risk across impact types, reflecting both the nature of the hazards and the economic and functional significance of each segment. A few key insights are highlighted below:
1. Dominance of Landslide- and Drought-Related Risks: Across the three localities, landslides and drought-induced ground instability consistently emerge as the most critical risks. In S2 (Pustý hrad slopes), landslide-related PRIs reach 4–5, indicating severe consequences for slopes, heritage sites, and power lines, while S3 (Neresnica) registers PRI = 3 for landslides, reflecting high sensitivity of vegetated slopes and culverts. Drought impacts are also pronounced, particularly in S2, where vegetation degradation and soil instability elevate the risk to an EXTREME level (PRI = 5).
2. Moderate but Persistent Risk from Heavy Precipitation: Both S1 (Zvolen Bypass Corridor (E77)) and S3 (Neresnica) show PRI = 2 for heavy precipitation, primarily linked to drainage overload, culvert failure, and localized flooding. While less severe than landslides, these impacts represent recurrent maintenance and operational challenges, especially considering the limited redundancy of road infrastructure in these corridors.
3. Temperature-Related Hazards: Heat waves and temperature variability contribute moderate PRI values (2–3) across all three localities. In S1 (Zvolen Bypass Corridor (E77)), heat waves are associated with rutting and deformation of road surfaces (PRI = 3), while in S3 (Neresnica) similar values are linked to pavement cracking and surface degradation. Though not extreme, these risks are expected to intensify over time and drive higher OPEX through increased maintenance needs.
4. Operational Risks Parallel Structural Risks: As in Level 2, the Zvolen case study demonstrates that operational disruptions (revenue-related impacts) closely follow the patterns of physical damage. For example, in S2 (Pustý hrad slopes), landslide-related disruptions to transport services, heritage accessibility, and power supply reach PRI = 3–4, mirroring CAPEX and OPEX damages. This reinforces the interdependence between asset condition and service continuity.
5. Lower Significance of Wind Hazards: Wind-related hazards generally yield low PRI values (1– 2) across all three segments, consistent with the relatively low hazard intensity of extreme wind events projected for the Zvolen region. While these risks are less critical compared to hydrometeorological hazards, their potential to act as compounding factors (e.g., in combination with storms or tree falls on power lines) suggests that they should not be fully overlooked.
6. Segment-Level Differentiation of Risks: The segment-specific assessment confirms distinct risk profiles: S1 (Zvolen Bypass Corridor (E77)) is exposed mainly to heavy rain and heat-related risks with low-to-moderate PRI values; S2 (Pustý hrad slopes) stands out as the highest-risk area due to slope instability, drought, and the concentration of vulnerable assets; while S3 (Neresnica) shows moderate risks, particularly from landslides and heavy rainfall affecting culverts and slopes.
In summary, the PRI analysis for Zvolen highlights the critical importance of hydrological and geomorphological hazards, particularly landslides and drought-related ground instability, while also acknowledging the cumulative effect of moderate risks from heavy precipitation and heat waves. These results provide a robust basis for prioritizing adaptation interventions, with S2 requiring urgent measures for slope stabilization and vegetation management, while S1 and S3 would benefit from drainage improvements, road surface reinforcement, and targeted maintenance planning.
