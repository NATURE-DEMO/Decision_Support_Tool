The Hazard Index (HI) at Level 3 for Zvolen has been calculated using downscaled climate projections, consistent with EURO-CORDEX methodologies, at a spatial resolution appropriate for localized analysis (10–50 km grid scale). Where possible, national datasets and site-specific indicator calculations were also incorporated to refine hazard estimates for the three localities. Multiple climate scenarios (RCP4.5, RCP8.5) and time horizons (mid-term: 2050; long-term: 2100) have been considered to capture the trajectory of climate hazard intensification.

The Level 3 hazard characterization for Zvolen reveals several critical insights. Heat waves emerge as one of the most dominant hazards, escalating from HIGH under mid-term scenarios to EXTREME under longterm projections, particularly under RCP8.5. This hazard is closely linked to increased risks of pavement deformation, wildfire ignition, and stress on slope vegetation. Droughts and water stress also show Page a marked increase, rising to VERY HIGH or EXTREME by 2100, with direct implications for ecosystem services, infrastructure durability, and wildfire susceptibility. Heavy precipitation and landslides represent another key hazard group: both intensify under RCP8.5, with landslides escalating to EXTREME in the long term due to slope saturation and soil instability. This has direct consequences for the Zvolen Bypass Corridor (E77) and Neresnica locality, where embankments, drainage systems, and culverts are particularly vulnerable. By contrast, cold waves and wind-related hazards show limited importance, with negligible or no projected changes, aligning with Level 2 findings. Wildfires, while less critical in the Zvolen Bypass Corridor (E77) and Neresnica localities, pose a serious medium-term and long-term threat at Pustý hrad slopes locality, where cultural heritage and tourism assets lie adjacent to forested areas prone to ignition. Overall, the Zvolen results confirm that temperature extremes, drought, flooding, and slope instability are the principal hazards requiring prioritisation in the Level 3 framework. These findings reinforce the need for targeted adaptation measures such as slope stabilisation, advanced drainage, and wildfire prevention, tailored to the different asset profiles of the three localities.
